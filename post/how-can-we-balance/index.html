How Can We Balance?

· 2023-11-07 · # IoT # PG Life

I was scratching my head, puzzled by the three different measurements on the screen at almost the same time. Did Professor Duncan's words in the first lesson, "Don't assume your measurements are valid" come to me so soon? A little distressed, I pulled the nail out of the plant and put it back in, praying that it would return to normal. As I watched the data changing on the screen and the nails going up and down, a thought flashed through my mind: my sensor was accurate, the problem was the moisture in the soil was not distributed uniformly!

To test my hypothesis, I filled one side of the soil with a large amount of water (the plant in the soil was a hyacinths, so I didn't have to worry about rotting roots due to excessive humidity!) ), simultaneously placed the sensor on the opposite side and measured the moisture value at that point, then moved the sensor to the water-filled side and recorded the moisture value on that side.

The above photo shows the different distances between two nails.

The figure above shows the value of soil moisture at different intervals between the two nails.

The experiment revealed that there was a large difference in the state of the soil's moisture between the two sides. Although my rudimentary experiment did not strictly control for all other variables (such as the spacing between the nails) to be consistent, the large data discrepancy still illustrates where the problem lies with this plant monitor. As I said in my presentation, the data provided by the plant monitor is difficult to accurately reflect the soil condition of the entire pot, due to the spacing between the pegs and the permeability of the soil. Imagine a scenario where you add a pump to your plant monitor to automate the watering process. If you place the sensor on the opposite side of the pump, it is likely that the pump will be turned on much more often than if it were placed on the same side of the pump, which would increase the chance of root rot (if you bought a hyacinths like I did, you might not have to worry about this). The solution to this is actually quite simple, place more sensors at intervals in the soil and use a weighted average (different weights are assigned depending on the distance of the sensor from the pump) as the basis for whether or not to turn the pump on. For example, in our plant monitor project, by default there is only one sensor assigned to each student (of course you can request more sensors!), at which point the issue of the cost of acquiring the sensor comes into play (in the plant monitor project, this cost can be thought of as the cost of negotiating with the professor to request additional sensors!). At this point, then, it extends to the issue I want to discuss today, how to balance the two factors of sensor cost as well as the quality of the data.

Connected Devices bridge the gap between humans and the environment by transforming small changes in the environment that are unnoticeable or unquantifiable into data that can be easily processed and understood by humans. They help us to better understand our environment, and based on these data, we can more accurately discover the secrets hidden in the environment. In addition, human beings can communicate their thoughts to the environment through the graphical and linguistic interfaces they are familiar with on connected devices, which can be said to be a translator for effective two-way communication between human beings and the environment. Humans no longer need to guess what is happening in the environment based on their own feelings, and the environment does not need to suffer from irrational human behaviour. The accuracy of sensor data can be interpreted as the ability of this translator to correctly understand the meaning of the both sides, and if there is a deviation, then it may result in a different outcome than expected. However, the increased accuracy of sensors is often accompanied by a significant increase in cost.

Taking the Complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) on mobile phones, which we often refer to as image sensor, as an example, the cost of camera components has been rising in recent years after all major mobile phone makers have started to improve the performance of their camera systems. According to Counterpoint's teardown analysis of Apple's iPhone series, for example, the cost of the camera system on the latest iPhone 15 Pro Max has increased by 34 percent compared to the previous-generation iPhone 14 Pro Max, due to the inclusion of a 5x optical fixed-focus lens (BoM Analysis, 2023). The iPhone 14 Pro Max, in turn, will cost another $6.3 more in camera cost compared to its predecessor due to a CMOS that is 65% larger in area (BoM, 2022). As the pixels as well as the area of the CMOS gradually increase, so does its cost. Other types of sensors have a similar trend, but this will lead to a steep rise in the difficulty of realisation for some projects with limited funding.

In a survey (Teh, Kempa-Liehr and Wang, 2020), the authors mention that due to a number of factors, most notably cost and difficulty of maintenance, most of the IoT systems trade off data accuracy for lower cost, that is, they use a large number of but low-cost sensors to collect data. This lower quality data is then processed by subsequent algorithms (e.g. PCA or ANN) to ensure its accuracy. However even using artificial neural networks, which are able to learn very well the specific patterns present in the data, to process erroneous data from sensors suffers from instability and even inaccuracy (Jäger et al., 2014). Of course, we cannot blame neural networks for all of this, because in practice, there are many reasons why sensors may lead to erroneous data, such as changes in voltage and current due to gradually decreasing battery power, and displacements due to external factors. So people started to study more complex and deeper neural networks, and even used the attention mechanism to control the accuracy (Zhang et al., 2022). Indeed, as the complexity of neural networks increases, the accuracy of the data also increases. However, the deeper the neural network, the more difficult it is to explain the technical black box that exists in the neural network (Zhang and Zhu, 2018), and therefore the source of much of the data as well as the changes cannot be directly explained, and there is no way to confirm the reliability of the data.

In order to better balance these existential trade-offs, I think a possible approach is to use regression analysis. For example, to balance the cost of the sensors with the accuracy of the data, we can first test the accuracy of the data from differently priced sensors and fit a function using a simple multi-layer perceptron (MLP). In practice, the expected funding for the project and the number of sensors required can be entered into the fitted function to obtain an approximate data accuracy and fine-tune the project setup accordingly to ensure an optimal balance between data accuracy and cost. It is also necessary to use error correction algorithms or interpretable neural networks to help analyse the reliability of the collected data. These algorithms can correct for unusual variations in the data due to external factors or simply remove outliers. As a result, the final data collected can be considered to be the most accurate within the budget.

Of course, as mankind's technological level advances, the cost of highly accurate sensors is bound to come down, just as the semiconductor industry is rapidly evolving. The cost of 7nm photolithography, which was cutting edge a few years ago, is now very acceptable. Perhaps in the near future, high-precision sensors will become affordable for everyone. When high-performance yet low-cost sensors are used in connected devices, human beings may be able to more easily grasp the changes in the environment, so that human beings and the environment is truly called an organic entity.
